Reactants

  • Triglyceride: Long-chain fatty acids from oils
  • NaOH: Strong base that breaks ester bonds
  • Heat: Accelerates the reaction process

Products

  • Soap (RCOONa): Sodium salt of fatty acids
  • Glycerin: Moisturizing byproduct
  • Water: Solvent and medium for reaction
1

Hydrolysis

NaOH breaks the ester bonds in triglycerides, splitting them into glycerol and fatty acid chains.

2

Neutralization

Fatty acids react with sodium ions (Na⁺) to form sodium salts—the soap molecules.

3

Completion

Glycerin separates as a byproduct. The soap is then diluted with water to create liquid soap.

Step-by-Step Guide

Experimental Procedure

Preparation of Reagents

Dissolve Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) flakes in distilled water. Warning: This reaction is exothermic (generates heat).

Mixing Phase

Slowly add the lye solution to the oil while stirring continuously. Maintain a steady mixing speed.

Addition of Catalysts

Add catalysts if required to speed up the reaction or enhance properties. Ensure even distribution.

Heating Process

Heat the mixture to 60-80°C. Monitor temperature closely to avoid overheating or separation.

Target Temp: 80°C

Observation

Observe the mixture thickening (trace). It should resemble pudding consistency.

Filtration

Filter the mixture to remove any undissolved particles or impurities.

Separation

Allow the soap to settle and separate if necessary (e.g., glycerin separation for certain methods).

Final Processing

Adjust pH to safe levels (9-10). Add fragrance or essential oils at this stage.

Quality Testing

Test for foam stability, clarity, and usage safety.
